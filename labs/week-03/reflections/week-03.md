## Prompts

1. What did you learn this week?
   >I learned how to identify the differences between the root, intermediate, and server certificates when looking at the command line output. I also learned that the command that renders the certificate info is not human-readable and has to be copied and pasted or exported to a notepad, which then can be interpreted into a human-readable format by the command prompt.

2. What concept was most challenging?
   >When I was looking at the output from commands that showed the certificates together in one output, I had a hard time understanding what I was looking at because I didn't realize that sometimes the root CA won’t appear in the output and you have to go download the .pem file.

3. Where does this concept appear in real-world systems?
   > Banks, internal portals, VPNs.

4. How would you explain this topic to a non-technical audience?
   > Certificates are like locks on doors that only open for the right key. They make sure that when your system connects to a website or server, it’s connecting to the real one, not a fake. The chain of trust is like checking that the lock comes from a trusted locksmith. Without it, anyone could make a fake lock and trick the system.

5. What questions remain?
   > I’m not confused about the concept itself; I’m more curious why some organizations let certificates expire or don’t have someone knowledgeable to fix issues.
